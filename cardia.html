Causal Inference

[ STORY ]

I analyzed observational data from the Karolinska Institute to investigate whether cardia cancer patients treated at high-volume hospitals have better survival outcomes than those at low-volume facilities. I successfully employed instrumental variables and propensity score methods to address confounding factors, revealing a modest positive effect of 0.04 years in survival when assigned to high-volume hospitals. My role as the analyst was to focus on assumption validation, propensity score modeling, and average causal effect estimation. This case study emphasizes the methodological rigor I applied (from SUTVA verification to subclass stratification) to extract causal insights from observational healthcare data.

Introduction

This experiment is based on an observational dataset from the Karolinska Institute of people who were diagnosed with cardia cancer in Sweden. We want to assess whether patients who received medical treatment from hospitals with high-volume of patients with cardia cancer have different survival rates than patients who received medical treatment from low-volume hospitals. This was an individual final project.

Assumptions

To conduct this experiment to determine if there’s a causal effect, we need to check if certain criteria are met. The first criteria is the SUTVA 1 and 2, and by looking at the dataset, both holds. SUTVA 1 holds because the treatment of whether the patients were diagnosed at a high- or low-volume hospital is clearly defined by a binary variable, where 1 is a high-volume and 0 is a low-volume hospital. SUTVA 2 also seems to hold because whether a patient gets diagnosed at a high- or low-volume hospital does not affect another patient’s potential outcome of the number of years of surviving after diagnosis. Although some might be concerned about SUTVA 2 because there are limited hospitals and doctors in Sweden, we are only looking at 158 patients, and the example above is a rare case, so I think it’s ok to assume that doctors will try their best to diagnose and treat the patient as accurately as possible. We can assume that both SUTVA 1 and 2 hold. We also need to check if we can assume unconfoundedness. Here, the diagnosing hospital is unconfounded, while the treating hospital is confounded. If we thought that the treating hospital was unconfounded, then we could have simply done an observational study on treating hospital, without the instrumental variables step. But since it is confounded, we will go through the following steps.

After cleaning some variables, I tried two methods: 1. estimating propensity scores for each unit in the study using the fitted values from a regression tree on the FromRuralArea and Male variables and 2. on all variables. Calculate the Average Causal Effect Estimate

To estimate the average causal effect, I first calculated p_c and the estimated ITT to calculate the ITT_c for each of the three subgroups and compared the p_a values in the end.

Results

For the first subgroup (propensity score = 0.238), the estimated ITT value is 0.075 with p_c equal to 0.75, leading to an ITT_c of 0.1. For the second group (propensity score = 0.475), the estimated ITT value is 0.0627 with p_c equal to 0.524, leading to an ITT_c of 0.1196. For the third group (propensity score = 0.658), the estimated ITT value is 0.023 with p_c equal to 0.383, leading to an ITT_c of 0.06. So, by weighing these subclasses, overall, the point estimate is 0.04200808 with the interval (-0.29, 0.2). This result means that the effect of being assigned to get treated at a high-volume hospital is 0.04, with a 95% possibility of this effect ranging as low as -0.29 and as high as 0.2. The overall years of survival after getting diagnosed would improve by 0.28 if patients with cardia cancer were treated at high-volume hospitals. This is the effect that the doctors would be interested in because they can control whether they open a spot in the hospital to treat a cardia cancer patient.

Limitations

The limitation of this experiment is the lack of information and details on the patients. Specifically, for the YearsSurvivingAfterDiagnosis variable, are we assuming that everyone is getting the treatment? When are they getting treated? If we know they survived for a year after being diagnosed, are we assuming all patients were treated within a year? Why would we be counting the number of years of surviving after getting diagnosed and not after getting treated if we want to see the effect of getting treated at a high-volume hospital versus a low-volume hospital? Some clarifications would be needed, and there were multiple assumptions made throughout. So, making sure that those are safe assumptions to make would be essential to make this result and interpretation more accurate.
